Summer School on Computational Imaging

The second ICCP Summer School on Computational Imaging will take place July 11–12, 2026, immediately before the ICCP 2026 main conference, at Princeton University. The summer school provides students, researchers, and practitioners from around the world the opportunity to attend hands-on courses on current research topics in computational imaging and computational photography.

Eligibility and Application Process

Applications have now concluded and we have sent out invitations to the successful applicants.

There are two participation options for the summer school, with different eligibility criteria and application processes.

Fellows: This option is available to everyone currently enrolled full-time in an undergraduate or graduate degree program, or officially starting in such a program in fall 2026. Each fellow will receive financial aid up to $1000 to cover travel, lodging, and registration costs. To apply, please complete the fellow application form.

Non-fellows: This option is available broadly to students, postdocs, and professionals in academia or industry. To apply, please complete the non-fellow application form.

Selection process: The ICCP 2026 organizing committee will select 25 fellows and 25 non-fellows from among eligible applicants to participate in the summer school.

Timeline: We will start processing applications on June 4, 23:59 ET, when we will start sending invitations to successful applicants on a rolling basis. However, we will continue accepting applications until June 30, 23:59 ET, or until all slots are filled.

Registration and Visa

Successful applicants will need to purchase a special registration to participate in the summer school, priced as follows.

  • Fellow: $150.
  • Student non-fellow or postdoc: $500
  • Non-student and non-postdoc: $1,500

If you plan to also attend the main conference, you will need to purchase a separate registration.

We strongly encourage school applicants that need a visa to enter the United States to register for the main conference as soon as possible (in parallel with their summer school application), so they can be issued visa invitation letters immediately. Unsuccessful applicants that do not plan to attend the main conference will be able to request a full refund of their registration until June 30, 2026. Successful applicants that have already registered for the main conference will have to pay only the difference in registration fees, depending on whether they plan to attend the main conference. We will email information on how to register to successful applicants with the summer school invitation.

Lodging and Meals

We have reserved 40 rooms at Princeton University's student residences as an affordable lodging option for successful student applicants. Information about how to reserve a room and how to pay for lodging will be emailed to successful applicants with the summer school invitation. Priority will be given to fellows. Students that prefer not to stay in the student residences will need to make their own lodging arrangements.

We will provide lunch and dinner during the two days of the summer school days.

Adaptive Optics: Principles and Applications

Adaptive optics (AO) enables optical systems to measure and correct aberrations in real time, improving image quality in astronomy, microscopy, and other imaging applications. In this course, students will first the basic principles and historical development of adaptive optics, and then build and operate a compact AO system consisting of a spatial light modulator, a wavefront sensor, and a camera. We will implement closed-loop aberration correction in two ways: 1) using an existing Hartmann wavefront sensor for sensor-based control; and 2) using image-quality optimization for sensor-less control without direct wavefront sensing. Through these two approaches, students will learn the basic hardware, calibration, control, and practical trade-offs of real AO systems.

Instructor: Mohit Gupta

Single-Photon Computational Imaging

Single-photon avalanche diodes (SPADs) are an emerging class of image sensors capable of detecting individual photons, at timing resolutions from picoseconds to nanoseconds. SPAD sensors are commonly used in active imaging applications such as lidar, and are becoming increasingly popular in compelling passive imaging applications. In this course, we provide an introduction to SPADs, how they capture light, their imaging applications, and the hardware trends that are influencing the future outlook of these sensors and research directions in this space. The course includes a strong hands-on component featuring a low-cost ($20) SPAD sensor used for active imaging, and a quarter-megapixel SPAD sensor used for passive imaging.

Instructor: Ioannis Gkioulekas

Interferometric Computational Imaging

This course will provide an introduction to interferometry, a classical imaging methodology with applications ranging from retinal scanning in ophthalmology, to coherent lidar in autonomous vehicles, and to gravitational wave detection in astronomy. More recently, interferometric computational imaging methods have enabled capabilities such as passive lidar and quadrillion FPS videos. We will learn about the physics of interferometry and coherent imaging, and emerging computational imaging applications. We will also get hands-on experience working with interferometric systems, by building, calibrating, and capturing data with a low-cost interferometer capable of 3D scanning at micrometer resolutions.
